洞穴剧场 — An Immersive Experience

Project Overview

One System, Infinite Narratives

Cave Theatre is an immersive installation themed around French prehistoric cave paintings, designed for commercial spaces, museums, and cultural tourism venues.

The experience spans approximately 150–200 square meters, comprising three sequential spaces with a total duration of about 30 minutes. Operations follow a continuous flow model: groups of 6 enter every 5 minutes, with a maximum of 18 visitors on-site at any time — as one group exits, another enters, with no fixed showtime slots. Upon entry, each visitor picks up a handheld oil lamp prop that accompanies them throughout.

At its core is a custom spatial sensing and projection system that tracks each oil lamp's position in real time, driving dynamic projection lighting and responsive content. The hardware and software architecture is built once and reused across deployments, with content replaced per project to support multi-venue replication.

Why Cave Paintings

A Thirty-Thousand-Year Mystery

The paintings at Chauvet Cave date back approximately 36,000 years; those at Lascaux are around 17,000 years old — a gap of nearly 20,000 years, roughly five times the distance between us and China's Sanxingdui civilization. Yet throughout this vast span of time, prehistoric humans continued to paint deep inside caves, until about 10,000 years ago this practice suddenly stopped. No one knows why. No one knows what these images meant. Were they ritual, record-keeping, a language entirely forgotten? These stories endured for thirty thousand years and then became eternal mysteries.

They have waited on the rock walls for thirty thousand years. Still no answers.

The prehistoric caves of southwestern France represent the most significant concentration of prehistoric art sites known today; UNESCO has designated several of them as World Heritage Sites. Yet these caves have been closed to the public one by one for conservation reasons — Lascaux has been off-limits since 1963, and Chauvet has been sealed since its discovery in 1994. What visitors around the world can see today are almost exclusively replicas produced by specialized institutions.

The French government invested over €57 million in the Lascaux IV replica center — the replica itself has become the official public form of this heritage. Today's technology allows replicas to go far beyond that: they can be interactive, responsive to visitor behavior, and deployable in any city. Geographic and spatial constraints are no longer barriers, and for the first time, the full potential of experiencing this heritage has opened up.

The Experience

Three Sequential Spaces

01

The Replica Gallery

approx. 12 × 5 m

One side of the gallery features 3–4 sections of cave wall replicas corresponding to real sites: the Lascaux Bulls Hall, Chauvet Cave, and the Rouffignac mammoth frieze. The replicas are fabricated from fiberglass with mineral coatings, faithfully reproducing the surface relief of actual rock.

Upon entering, the hall is near-total darkness. Visitors pick up an oil lamp from the lamp stand — the lamp lights up in their hand. Beside the stand sits a stone bowl, empty, waiting to be lit. Visitors instinctively bring their lamp close; the simulated flame inside the bowl ignites, flickering irregularly in warm tones. At the same moment, the projection gate at the gallery entrance slowly opens. This gesture requires no instruction — it simultaneously delivers a ritualistic beginning and teaches visitors that the lamp can illuminate things in the space.

Only where the lamp shines does light appear. The rest remains dark — exactly the same condition faced by the prehistoric painters who entered these caves with nothing but one lamp, able to see only within that circle of light.

Along the gallery walls, 5–6 stone bowls are embedded in the replica surfaces. Any visitor approaching alone causes a bowl to illuminate — the simulated flame LED produces a natural, irregular flicker while lighting up the adjacent section of cave painting: colors deepen, details emerge. The more bowls lit along the corridor, the closer the space comes to resembling the working conditions of the prehistoric painters.

Sensory Environment

At the entrance, the scent is limestone and mineral, carrying the characteristic coolness of caves. This is controlled by a scent diffusion unit at the entry, completely isolated from outdoor air. The sound system maintains a deep ambient layer throughout the gallery — the resonance of rock walls, the faint drip of distant water at intervals, the occasional whisper of airflow — not forming distinct sound events, but giving the space the right texture. Footsteps here sound different from a typical exhibition hall; visitors instinctively move more softly. These three sensory layers — scent, darkness, sound — together establish a genuine sensory space.

Real-time Lighting System

The lamp's position is tracked in real time by UWB sensors; the projection system generates corresponding illumination across the replica surface based on the coordinates. Because the replicas have genuine physical relief, light arriving from different angles produces different bas-relief shadows — the rock texture is real, not a flat simulation.

There is one type of experience from real caves that is very difficult to replicate in static displays: many cave paintings incorporate incised lines that are nearly invisible under direct light, appearing only when light grazes the surface at a low, oblique angle. This was intentional on the part of the prehistoric painters — the image reveals itself according to the angle of light, not as a fixed picture. We overlay a real-time projection layer onto the replica surfaces so that the direction of the lamp directly affects the direction of light and shadow on the wall. As visitors move their lamp, certain details on the rock face gradually emerge from darkness. This experience is normally available only to those walking through real caves holding a torch — we bring it into the exhibition space.

Two Types of Trigger Events

  • ①
    Whispered Commentary

    When a lamp lingers in front of a section of cave painting for more than 8 seconds, spatial audio is triggered from the direction of the painting — not a broadcast, but a close whisper describing a specific detail of that painting. The language uses invented syllables with rhythmic and tonal variation that resembles real speech in structure, but belongs to no existing language. Listeners cannot understand the literal content but can feel the emotion and intent. After the sound ends, the painting area briefly glows with an ember-like highlight, then fades.

  • ②
    Animal Activation

    When a lamp comes very close to a painted animal, an outline-tracing animation is triggered: a luminous line begins at the head and travels along the contour for 2–3 seconds, covering the entire body as if being redrawn, then emits one ember pulse before fading within 2 seconds. The Lascaux horse has six legs, the aurochs eight — this was the prehistoric painter's way of expressing movement through multiple limbs. When the six-legged horse is triggered, its six legs are traced in sequence corresponding to the rhythm of its stride; the sense of motion emerges naturally through the tracing process.

At the far end of Scene 1, when a visitor walks to the exit area and pauses, a floor projection of footprints appears — a set of human footprints and animal tracks extending from the gallery end toward the entrance of Scene 2, guiding visitors to follow.

Hidden Content: Geometric Symbols

Real cave paintings contain numerous geometric patterns of unknown meaning — spirals, dot arrays, grid-like symbols — that scholars have been unable to explain. These same symbols are hidden in the replica walls of the exhibition space, but are completely invisible at normal lighting angles. Only when an oil lamp is brought very close at an extremely low, oblique angle do the symbols emerge from the surface. A subtle audio cue signals the discovery; the symbol briefly glows, then fades. A total of 5 symbols are hidden across the three scenes, distributed in different locations.

02

The Amphitheatre

approx. 8–10 m diameter, 4 m ceiling

The circular space features an unbroken 360° projection surface. Four directions correspond to four seasons, running simultaneously:

Spring

A mare and her foal walk out of the depth of the image; a herd of deer migrates to the right

Summer

Bison gather in dense herds, bulls massive in scale, with a school of salmon swimming below

Autumn

Two stags face each other, antlers fully spread, locked in confrontation

Winter

Animals sparse, hand stencils layered on rock, geometric symbols scattered throughout

All four seasons coexist within this circular space. Visitors move toward whichever direction draws them, entering that season's tableau.

The animals on the walls appear to float on the rock surface; when a lamp approaches, they cast shadows that move with the light source — calculated in real time, following the visitor's lamp. Each species responds differently to light: the foal stops curiously and turns its head toward the light source; the nearest bison in the herd reacts first with alertness that ripples back through the group; the stag slowly approaches a visitor who holds their lamp steady and still. In the Winter section, hand stencils glow faintly as a lamp approaches; visitors can hold their hand close to the wall and a camera captures the hand shape, layering their own print beside the ancient ones — it fades slowly after about 60 seconds.

Real-time projection test footage

At the center of the circular space, a semi-transparent projection shows several prehistoric human figures. They sit around a small fire — looking down at the flames, tracing shapes in the ground with their fingers, standing still listening to distant sounds. They do not look at visitors, do not respond to any action, but simply exist in their own time. Visitors walk around them, illuminating them with their lamps.

03

Exit: VR Lookout

approx. 4 × 4 m

The exit area features 6 suspended VR viewers arranged in a semicircle, forming a small observation platform. Wrapped in dark metal instrument housings, each visitor presses their eyes to a viewer and looks — like using a telescope — for about 40 seconds. All 6 enter and exit together, producing no wait.

Inside the viewer is a 360° stereoscopic video: the viewpoint stands at the mouth of the cave, looking out over open prehistoric terrain — a broad river valley, amber grassland, a herd of wild horses moving slowly in the middle distance, a mammoth group with young crossing the plain far away. Looking back, faint firelight still flickers inside the cave.

No narration. No commentary. Just that world, quietly present.

The scent here has changed — no longer limestone, but wet grass and cold air. A return station is positioned at the exit. Visitors place their lamp back, and it goes dark.

Exit: Digital Collectibles

After returning the lamp, visitors scan a QR code at the exit to enter a WeChat mini-program and claim their digital collectible from the experience. The collectible system operates on three tiers:

  • D
    Daily Edition

    Every visitor receives that day's base collectible upon entry, featuring a cave animal motif. 12 themes rotate monthly throughout the year (wild horse, bison, mammoth, cave bear, woolly rhinoceros…), with limited editions released on solstices, equinoxes, and other special dates. Different visitors receive different collectibles depending on their visit date, creating a natural incentive to collect and return.

  • E
    Explorer Edition

    Awarded for discovering hidden geometric symbols. Each carries a discovery sequence number ("You are the 237th person to find this symbol"). Lower numbers are rarer; early visitors naturally acquire greater scarcity value.

  • C
    Complete Set Reward

    Collecting all of the current month's animal editions or finding all 5 geometric symbols unlocks a full animated cave scene collectible. All collectibles can be shared directly to social media, generating organic word-of-mouth.

Sensory & Interaction Design

Seen, Heard, Smelled

Temperature

The space is maintained at approximately 17–18°C, cool like a cave without being uncomfortable. The VR exit area is slightly warmer, giving the body a sense of "leaving the cave."

Scent · Gallery

Wet calcium carbonate + chalk mineral note + faint moist earth. A cold-mist diffuser at the entry creates a scent threshold — step inside and the atmosphere shifts.

Scent · Exit

Petrichor / geosmin + cold outdoor air. A complete contrast to the gallery; VR visuals and olfactory experience sync.

Sound Design

Gallery-wide ambient soundscape: an ultra-low-frequency cave resonance (cave tone) as the foundation, felt more than heard. Irregular water drips spaced 12–25 seconds apart, with long reverb tails whose length hints at spatial depth. A very subtle airflow sound, as if an opening somewhere is breathing.

  • F
    Bowl Ignition

    A soft crackle of fat burning for 1–2 seconds, then a stable, low-frequency hum merges into the ambient soundscape and continues for 2–3 minutes before fading as the flame dims.

  • A
    Animal Interaction

    Directional spatial audio with sound source precisely aligned to the painting's position. Bison: a low chest-deep exhalation. Horse: a light, tense snort. Sound comes from the direction of the painting, not from speakers.

  • W
    Whispered Commentary

    Uses an invented syllable language — rhythmic and tonally varied, structured like real speech, but belonging to no existing language. Visitors cannot understand the literal content but feel the emotion and tone. Sound comes from the painting's position; the painting briefly glows afterward.

  • S
    Hidden Symbol Discovery

    A single strike of stone on stone — not repeated, dry and brief. Like an ancient signal of response.

Scene 2's circular space features a dedicated ambient soundscape in each of the four directions: Spring — trickling water and distant birdsong; Summer — dense, low-frequency insect chorus; Autumn — intermittent wind and dry leaf rustle; Winter — near silence, with the occasional distant sound of ice cracking. As visitors move through the circular space, the sounds mix naturally by position. The central figures occupy a zone of continuous, barely perceptible fire sounds and an unintelligible low murmur.

Visual Interaction Language: Outline Tracing Animation

When a cave painting animal is activated, a unified outline-tracing animation plays: beginning at the head of the animal's silhouette, a luminous line travels along the contour over 2–3 seconds, covering the whole body as if someone is redrawing the line with light. After completion, the entire outline performs one ember pulse (a slight rise and fall in brightness), then slowly fades within 2 seconds, returning to the painting's original state.

This animation carries two meanings: first, "this line was originally drawn in exactly this way," and second, "your lamplight has brought it back to life." The six-legged horse trigger is the fullest version — its six legs are traced in sequence corresponding to the rhythm of each stride, and the sense of motion emerges naturally through the tracing process.

Real Estate Integration

Activating Silent Spaces

Utilizing Vacant Space

Commercial real estate contains a persistent category of hard-to-lease space: basement levels, gallery end-caps, corner locations in large-footprint shopping centers. What these spaces share — enclosed, dim, far from main circulation — is precisely what immersive experiences require.

Cave Theatre requires only approximately 150–200 square meters, and can be integrated into such locations without disrupting the existing commercial layout. Hardware installation per venue costs approximately ¥100K–150K; from a ticket revenue recovery standpoint, the hardware investment can be recouped in roughly 2–3 months, after which the venue generates continuous net income.

From the property owner's perspective, this converts a zero-revenue or low-revenue floor area into a zone of stable daily output, while bringing foot traffic to the entire property.

Traffic Value at Project Launch

New commercial openings, sales center launches for residential developments, cultural tourism recruitment phases — these moments all require rapid generation of visits and buzz. Cave Theatre's visual environment (dark space, torchlight, animal murals) has high social media shareability, making it suitable as a limited-time experience to generate initial heat. The installation can be deployed in 2–3 weeks and relocated after the event concludes.

Cultural Amenity Impact on Asset Value

Commercial complexes with stable cultural programming show rental and sales premiums of approximately 15–25% in multiple market case studies. This effect is especially pronounced for premium residential and upper-mid-tier commercial projects. A continuously operating immersive cultural space can serve as the cultural anchor for an entire development's positioning.

Scalability

One Foundation, Infinite Themes

The system operates on two tiers: infrastructure (hardware, positioning, rendering framework) and content (murals, animals, scene design).

Infrastructure is 100% reused across deployments; only content is newly produced each time. Content costs represent approximately 25–35% of total per-deployment cost.

From the second deployment onward, hardware need not be re-purchased, and accumulated installation and calibration experience significantly shortens deployment timelines. As projects accumulate, marginal costs continue to decline, and profit margins at the same revenue scale progressively improve.

The core prop system (oil lamp + UWB positioning + projection response) is not dependent on the cave theme — it can be transplanted to other narrative contexts. Examples: in a shadow puppet experience, visitors hold a light source that determines which puppet is illuminated and from which projection angle; or for a nighttime garden tour, the lamp determines how plants, architecture, and water respond. The mechanism remains the same; content and form are redesigned for each context.

Academic Partnership & Cultural Exchange

Connecting Two Worlds

Sites such as Lascaux, Chauvet, and Rouffignac are national heritage properties managed by the French Ministry of Culture, and are also subjects of ongoing academic research. Institutions including the University of Bordeaux and the CNRS (Centre National de la Recherche Scientifique) have long-established expertise in prehistoric art.

We plan to engage with the French university system, inviting relevant scholars to participate in the project as advisors or partners. The practical value of academic collaboration is threefold: professional institutional backing for content accuracy; a credible credential when seeking authorization or accreditation from French cultural institutions; and access to French university and research networks that can support the project's reach within European cultural circles.

At the content level, this project has a natural Sino-French cultural intersection: French prehistoric heritage at its core, re-presented through digital technology by a Chinese team, operating in Chinese cities. This structure itself can serve as a concrete example of Sino-French cultural exchange, facilitating dialogue within the cultural institutions and official channels of both countries.

In the long term, after completing prototype validation in the Chinese market, we aim to bring the system and content to venues in France, creating a two-way flow: Chinese audiences experience the French prehistoric world; French audiences experience a Chinese team's contemporary interpretation of that same history.

This collaboration framework is not limited to the cave painting project. Other invitro projects involving cultural content or operations in both China and France can be incorporated into the same academic partnership and cultural exchange structure, forming an ongoing relationship rather than a one-off project engagement.

Market Timing

The Entry Window Is Opening

AI is lowering the cost of producing digital content. Animation, sound design, scene creation — work that previously required large teams can now be accomplished with AI tools in a fraction of the time, at less than half the production cost of three years ago.

For us, this is favorable: lower content production thresholds mean declining costs per deployment, making the commercial model increasingly viable.

At the same time, the proliferation of AI is making screen-based content ever richer and ever cheaper. Audiences can access large volumes of high-quality visual content at home, which paradoxically makes "must-be-there" physical experiences rarer and more valuable. Cave Theatre's experience depends on real physical space, real physical props, and real bodily presence — none of which digital content can substitute.

Now is a particularly suitable moment to enter this direction: AI tools are mature enough to help us reduce production costs, but large-scale competition in this category of physical experience has not yet emerged.

Key Risks

Replica Fabrication Cost Variability

The price range for fiberglass wall replicas is wide, depending on precision and manufacturer. A simplified version can be used in the prototype phase to control costs; supplier and pricing will be confirmed before the commercial deployment.

Visitor Volume Forecast Uncertainty

Ticket revenue projections assume stable utilization rates. Actual operations in the initial period may include a ramp-up phase with revenue below steady state for the first two months. It is recommended to simultaneously test pricing and reservation conversion rates during the prototype validation phase.

Venue Partnership Negotiation Timeline

Negotiations with museums or commercial properties typically take 2–4 months. Commercial space partnerships tend to move faster and can be prioritized as the target for the first deployment.

Appendix A: Technical Architecture

Core System Architecture

[UWB Anchors × 6–8, ceiling-mounted] ↓ real-time coordinates (UDP/OSC) [Main PC: Windows + RTX 4080] Unity — behavioral logic + projection lighting render ↓ KlakSpout (GPU VRAM direct transfer) Resolume Arena — multi-projector blending output ↓ DisplayPort × 4–6 [Wall projectors] [Secondary PC: Mac Mini] QLab — spatial audio + footprint sequence + DMX lighting ↓ OSC bidirectional communication with main PC [VR Viewers] Quest 3 standalone, Skybox VR playing 360° stereoscopic video

Oil Lamp Prop

Exterior modeled on Paleolithic fat lamp forms, resin 3D-printed shell, containing:

  • Positioning module ESP32 + DW1000 UWB, accuracy ±15–30cm
  • Light source 2700K warm white LED, slight irregular flicker
  • Battery 5000mAh, ~20 hours runtime
  • Unit cost ~¥650; first batch 12–15 units

Projection Lighting Principle

The replica surfaces have genuine physical relief. The system builds a corresponding 3D model for each replica segment, renders via a virtual projector camera in Unity, and outputs an image that geometrically matches the physical surface when projected. The lamp position serves as a virtual point light source, calculating in real time the direction of illumination on the rock surface — raised areas catch light, recessed areas cast shadows, and the effect shifts as the lamp angle changes.
